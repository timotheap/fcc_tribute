In the heart of Brussels, in a house Harry Potter and the like wouldn't have shunned, there is a private school that teaches the ancient techniques of decoration. Founded at the end of the XIXth century, this private school (Higher education) teaches marbling and woodgraining, murals, and other decorative techniques.

The facade itself is a work of art. The late M. Van Der Kelen once painted every single brick, one by one, to obtain the beautiful patina of old baked bricks.

The door and the windows are decorated, gilded dragons spit out volutes of iron, and it is only a prelued to what awaits the visitor.

It is worth entering the school if only to have a glimpse of the work of art on the walls of the hall. Large marblings, painted by M. Van Der Kelen and his students a long time ago, spread the entire length of the hall. Visitors can only wish they had more time and space to take the measure of this masterpiece.

Students vow to one day be able to paint like that, but marbling is a craft and it takes time and practice, and of course...talent.

It was first intended for house painters who had little work in winter. This corresponded to a period a six months, the duration of the training. It hasn't changed, though few house painters now specialise in those techniques. Marbling and woodgraining is no longer a must-have in the tools of the painter. Instead, artists and other crafstmen have taken over and keep the tradition alive.

Over 40 hours a week, for six months. Marbling (Grand Antique, Portor, Sarrancolin, Jaune de Sienne,...) and Woodgraining (chestnut, oak, mahogany, pine,...), murals (mouldings, skies), patinas (bronze, terracotta,..)...But also perspective, drawing, preparation,...

The training is intense, demanding. Students often stay up late in the night to finish their panels.

All the material is included in the tuition: oil paint, linseed oil, paper, and brushes.

The training is entirely based on observation followed by practice.

Ms. Van Der Kelen teaches in English, French and Flemish.

Traditional technique in oil paint.

Oil paint is favoured for its infinite possibilities of transparency and fine layering.

Students will learn how to prepare their own paint with linseed oil and pigments. Other decorative techniques for patinas include the use of beer.
